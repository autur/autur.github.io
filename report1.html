As part of the survey, you completed the Big Five Aspect Scale, a measure of the five major dimensions of personality and their aspects:

  • Openness/Intellect: Openness and Intellect
  • Conscientiousness: Industriousness and Orderliness
  • Agreeableness: Compassion and Politeness
  • Extraversion: Enthusiasm and Assertiveness
  • Neuroticism: Withdrawal and Volatility

In this report, you will find your rankings on these traits, along with descriptions of each trait, its advantages and disadvantages, and the average differences in personality between men and women. The rankings are in the form of percentiles, ranging from 0 to 100, which indicate how you rank on any given personality trait compared to other individuals from the general US population (i.e., people of all ages, sexes, ethnicities, etc.).

To help you understand how to interpret your percentile scores, imagine that you are in a room with 100 random individuals and we rank order everyone according to their score on trait X. If you score in the 60th percentile on that trait, it means that you would be higher on the trait than 59 of the individuals in the room, and conversely you would be lower on the trait than 40 of them. The higher your percentile score is, the more you exhibit the trait relative to other people.

Keep in mind that each personality trait and aspect (and your relative position with respect to them) has advantages and disadvantages. It is for that reason that variation exists in the human population: there is a niche for each personality configuration. Much of what constitutes success in life is therefore the consequence of finding the place in relationships, work and personal commitment that corresponds to your unique personality structure

Also note that your scores can be somewhat influenced by the state of mind you were in as you took the survey, particularly how well you were paying attention and responded thoughtfully to each question, and how moody and self-critical you were.

If you find that many of your scores are in the extreme ranges (0s and 90s), this may be due to an extreme responding bias, which means that you used the 'strongly agree' and 'strongly disagree' options much more frequently than others did. On the other hand, many people show the opposite bias of almost never using the extreme options (e.g., using only 'agree' and 'disagree') and that is also less than ideal.

There are also cultural differences in response styles, which may be especially relevant to you if you come from a non-Western culture. This article, for example, provides an explanation for why East Asian individuals show a more moderate response style than Westerners. And this recent article provides an explanation for why people from individualist cultures respond more extremely than those from collectivist cultures.

After reading this report, you may find yourself asking, "OK, so what do these scores mean?". The short and simple answer is that you should regard this report as an opportunity to learn more about yourself. Most people are able to describe their personality in some detail but have only a vague sense of how they compare to other individuals. Having this kind of information can help you form more a more accurate model of the average personality and your relation to it. Speaking more broadly, the more you know about yourself and the more accurate your self-knowledge, the better you'll be at (1) making choices that are congruent with your personality, and (2) identifying dysfunctional patterns of behavior that you may want to change.
